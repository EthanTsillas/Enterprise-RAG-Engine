Document Title: Aquaculture as a Response to Overfishing

Aquaculture, or fish farming, now produces more than 50% of seafood consumed globally for the first time in history as of 2022. Global aquaculture production reached 90.6 million metric tons in 2022, valued at 281.5 billion dollars. China is the dominant aquaculture producer, responsible for approximately 59% of global farmed seafood production. Salmon farming is concentrated in Norway, Chile, Canada, and Scotland, producing approximately 2.7 million metric tons annually. Tilapia is the most widely farmed fish globally in terms of number of producing countries. Shrimp aquaculture in Southeast Asia, particularly in Vietnam, Thailand, and Indonesia, has driven conversion of mangrove forests, with 38% of global mangrove loss attributed to shrimp farming. Farmed salmon require between 1.2 and 5 kilograms of wild fish as feed per kilogram of salmon produced, depending on feed composition. Sea lice infestations on farmed salmon cause significant mortality in both farmed and wild salmon populations. Recirculating aquaculture systems (RAS) eliminate ocean pollution and sea lice risks but require 4 to 10 times more energy than open-net cage farming. Shellfish farming including oysters, mussels, and clams is considered one of the most sustainable forms of aquaculture because shellfish filter feed on plankton and require no added food inputs.
