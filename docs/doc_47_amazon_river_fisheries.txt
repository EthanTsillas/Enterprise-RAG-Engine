Document Title: Amazon River and Freshwater Fisheries

The Amazon River basin contains the greatest freshwater fish diversity on Earth, with over 3,000 documented species, representing approximately 10% of all known vertebrate species. Freshwater fish provide 30 to 60% of animal protein intake for rural communities in the Brazilian Amazon. The arapaima (Arapaima gigas), one of the world's largest freshwater fish at up to 3 meters and 200 kilograms, was hunted nearly to extinction in accessible river systems by the 1990s. Community-based management of arapaima by the Mamiraua Sustainable Development Reserve, beginning in the 1990s, enabled population recovery from near extinction to over 140,000 individuals in managed areas by 2015, demonstrating the effectiveness of indigenous-led conservation. The pirarucu (another common name for arapaima) is now commercially harvested under strict community quota systems that have created economic incentives for conservation. Deforestation in the Amazon affects river hydrology and water temperature, degrading fish habitat across the basin. An estimated 60% of the Amazon's fish species require flooded forest habitat during some part of their life cycle, making forest conservation essential for fisheries sustainability. The Madeira River giant catfish and dourada migrate over 5,000 kilometers to spawn at the base of the Andes mountains, a journey disrupted by hydroelectric dams. Brazil, Peru, and Colombia manage the Amazon's fisheries under the Amazon Cooperation Treaty Organization but coordination is inconsistent. Illegal gold mining in the Amazon contaminates rivers with mercury, bioaccumulating in fish and threatening the health of indigenous communities who depend on fish protein.
