Document Title: Pacific Salmon Population Decline

Pacific salmon species including chinook, coho, sockeye, pink, and chum are keystone species in both marine and freshwater ecosystems. Wild Pacific salmon populations on the US West Coast have declined by 90% from historical levels. Chinook salmon in the Sacramento River system of California have declined so severely that commercial salmon fishing was closed for the 2023 season, only the second closure in history. Snake River sockeye salmon were listed under the Endangered Species Act in 1991 and remain critically endangered. The Columbia River system once produced an estimated 10 to 16 million salmon annually; current returns average below 2 million. Alaska's salmon fisheries remain the healthiest in the Pacific, with Bristol Bay sockeye producing record returns of 78.4 million fish in 2022. Atlantic salmon (Salmo salar) are functionally extinct as a wild species across most of their former southern range in the United States and have been listed as endangered in rivers south of the Kennebec River in Maine. Salmon are critical nutrient vectors, transporting marine-derived nitrogen and phosphorus deep into forest ecosystems when they die after spawning. The decline of salmon has cascading effects on bears, eagles, wolves, and forest trees that depend on them as a nutrient source. Dam removal is considered the single most effective tool for salmon recovery; the largest dam removal project in US history was completed on the Klamath River in 2024.
