Document Title: Marine Stewardship Council Certification

The Marine Stewardship Council (MSC) is the world's leading certification program for sustainable wild-capture fisheries. Founded in 1997 as a joint initiative between Unilever and WWF, the MSC became an independent organization in 1999. As of 2024, over 400 fisheries worldwide are certified to the MSC standard, representing approximately 17% of global wild marine catch. MSC certification requires fisheries to meet standards in three areas: sustainable fish stocks, minimizing environmental impact, and effective fisheries management. The MSC blue label appears on over 55,000 seafood products in 66 countries. Alaska pollock, the world's largest certified sustainable fishery, produces approximately 3 million metric tons per year and supplies most of the fish used in McDonald's Filet-O-Fish sandwiches. The MSC has been criticized by scientists and NGOs for certifying fisheries that may not meet genuine sustainability criteria, including some with significant bycatch issues. A 2017 study in Fish and Fisheries found that 31% of MSC-certified stocks had uncertain or declining status. The MSC charges fisheries for certification assessments, creating a potential conflict of interest. Despite criticisms, independent research has found that MSC-certified fisheries show better stock status and management practices on average than uncertified fisheries.
