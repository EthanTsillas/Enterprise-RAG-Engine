Document Title: Community-Based Fisheries Management

Community-based fisheries management (CBFM) involves local fishing communities in setting and enforcing rules for sustainable use of local marine resources. Territorial use rights in fisheries (TURFs) grant specific communities exclusive rights to defined fishing areas, incentivizing sustainable management. Japan's sea area management system, which has operated for over a century, allocates coastal fishing territories to cooperative associations of fishers and is credited with maintaining relatively healthy inshore stocks. Chile implemented a TURF system in 1991 for benthic resources including sea urchins and shellfish, resulting in stock recovery in managed areas. Hawaii's traditional ahupuaa system, which managed entire watershed-to-reef ecosystems, maintained sustainable fisheries for over 1,000 years before Western contact. Locally managed marine areas (LMMAs) in Fiji, Vanuatu, and Solomon Islands have documented recovery of fish populations inside closed areas within 3 to 5 years of establishment. Mexico's community-based lobster fishery in Baja California, managed by cooperative FEDECOOP, has maintained sustainable yields for over 70 years and holds MSC certification. CBFM is most effective when communities have clear tenure rights, self-governance authority, monitoring capacity, and access to outside technical support. Poverty and food insecurity undermine CBFM by creating pressure to harvest beyond sustainable limits, particularly during economic shocks. Indigenous fishing rights recognized under national and international law provide legal foundation for community management of traditional fishing territories in Canada, New Zealand, and Australia.
