Document Title: Kelp Forest Decline and Fisheries Impact

Kelp forests are among the most productive marine ecosystems on Earth, supporting diverse communities of fish, invertebrates, and marine mammals. California's kelp forests have declined by approximately 95% along the northern coast since 2014 due to a combination of sea star wasting disease, marine heat waves, and sea urchin population explosions. Sea urchins, which graze on kelp holdfasts, proliferated after the die-off of their primary predator, sunflower sea stars, caused by sea star wasting disease. Kelp forests along the coast of Tasmania declined by 95% between the 1940s and 2010s as warming waters favored urchin barrens over kelp. Norway and Japan have conducted urchin culling programs to facilitate kelp forest recovery. Southern California kelp forests are threatened by warm water intrusions and kelp harvest, with commercial kelp harvesting producing approximately 100,000 metric tons per year from California. Kelp forests support commercially important species including rockfish, lingcod, cabezon, sea urchins, and abalone. Red abalone populations in California collapsed when their kelp food source disappeared; the commercial abalone fishery was closed indefinitely in 2018 and the recreational fishery was closed in 2018 and has not fully reopened. Kelp provides critical structural habitat and food for over 700 species in California coastal waters. Restoration experiments using urchin removal and kelp transplanting have shown promising results in creating new kelp patches within 18 months.
