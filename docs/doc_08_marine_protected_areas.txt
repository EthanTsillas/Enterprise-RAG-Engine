Document Title: Marine Protected Areas and Fisheries Recovery

Marine protected areas (MPAs) are designated ocean zones where fishing and other extractive activities are restricted or prohibited. As of 2023, approximately 8.2% of the global ocean is designated as protected, covering about 30 million square kilometers. The 30x30 initiative, adopted at the COP15 biodiversity summit in December 2022, commits nations to protecting 30% of land and ocean by 2030. Fully protected no-take MPAs show the strongest recovery results, with fish biomass inside no-take zones averaging 670% higher than in unprotected areas. The Cabo Pulmo National Park in Mexico, established in 1995, saw a 463% increase in total fish biomass within 10 years of becoming a no-take reserve. Spillover effects from MPAs, where fish populations overflow boundaries into adjacent fishing zones, have been documented to increase catches for nearby fishers by 18 to 46%. Currently only 2.9% of the global ocean is fully protected as no-take zones. The Great Barrier Reef Marine Park covers 344,400 square kilometers and includes both protected and open fishing zones. Poorly enforced MPAs, sometimes called paper parks, provide little ecological benefit despite formal designation. Effective MPA enforcement typically requires satellite monitoring, patrol vessels, and community support from local fishing communities.
