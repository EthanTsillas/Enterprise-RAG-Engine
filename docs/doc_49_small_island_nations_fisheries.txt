Document Title: Small Island Developing States and Fisheries

Small island developing states (SIDS) are among the most dependent nations on fisheries for food security, government revenue, and employment. Pacific Island nations including Kiribati, Tuvalu, Marshall Islands, and Federated States of Micronesia earn between 30 and 80% of their government revenue from tuna fishing license fees paid by distant-water fishing nations. The Pacific Islands Forum Fisheries Agency manages fishing access agreements across 17 member nations, negotiating collectively to secure better terms than individual nations could achieve alone. The Parties to the Nauru Agreement, eight Pacific Island nations, implemented a vessel day scheme in 2012 that limits the total number of fishing days sold annually, creating a cap-and-trade system for fishing effort that has significantly increased license revenues. Kiribati's exclusive economic zone covers 3.5 million square kilometers, one of the largest in the world relative to land area, making fisheries income critical to the nation's economy. Maldives earns approximately 15% of GDP from fisheries, primarily pole-and-line caught skipjack tuna sold at a premium for its low bycatch. Fiji's inshore fisheries have declined by an estimated 50% since 1990 due to overfishing, threatening the food security of rural coastal communities. Climate change threatens to submerge low-lying Pacific Island nations entirely, destroying their exclusive economic zones along with their primary source of sovereign income. The Locally Managed Marine Area network in the Pacific has established over 700 community marine protected areas across 15 countries, covering approximately 6 million hectares. Caribbean SIDS face severe degradation of their coral reef fisheries from a combination of overfishing, coastal development, and climate bleaching, threatening both food security and tourism-dependent economies.
