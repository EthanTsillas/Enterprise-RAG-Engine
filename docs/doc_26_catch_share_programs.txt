Document Title: Catch Share Programs and Fisheries Reform

Catch shares, also called individual transferable quotas (ITQs), allocate specific portions of the total allowable catch to individual fishers or fishing companies. New Zealand introduced the world's first comprehensive ITQ system in 1986, covering 30 fish species. Iceland implemented ITQs for herring in 1975 and extended the system to most fisheries by 1990, contributing to the recovery of its cod stocks. The US Bering Sea pollock fishery adopted a catch share program in 2008; within five years, fuel use per pound of fish caught dropped by 40% and stock health improved. Catch shares reduce the race-to-fish dynamic that causes fishers to maximize short-term catch before others do, incentivizing more careful, selective fishing. Critics of catch shares argue that they lead to consolidation of fishing rights among large corporations, displacing small-scale fishing communities. In Alaska, catch shares in the halibut and sablefish fishery transformed the season from a two-day derby in dangerous conditions to a year-round fishery, dramatically improving safety and fish quality. A 2008 study in Science analyzing 11,135 fisheries found that catch share fisheries were half as likely to collapse as non-catch share fisheries. Chile's catch share system for industrial fisheries has been criticized for excluding artisanal fishers from quota allocation. Effective catch share programs require accurate stock assessments, strong enforcement, and equitable initial allocation to achieve both ecological and social sustainability goals.
