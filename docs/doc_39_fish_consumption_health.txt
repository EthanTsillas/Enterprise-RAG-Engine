Document Title: Fish Consumption Health Impacts and Mercury Contamination

Fish provides essential nutrition including omega-3 fatty acids, high-quality protein, iodine, and vitamin D for approximately 3.3 billion people globally. Omega-3 fatty acids (EPA and DHA) found in fatty fish reduce cardiovascular disease risk, support brain development in fetuses and infants, and have anti-inflammatory properties. Mercury contamination in seafood poses a significant public health concern, particularly for pregnant women and children. Mercury enters the ocean primarily through coal burning and industrial emissions and biomagnifies up the food chain, concentrating in large predatory fish. Shark, swordfish, king mackerel, tilefish, and bigeye tuna have the highest average mercury concentrations of commercially consumed seafood. The US FDA and EPA advise pregnant women to avoid high-mercury fish and limit consumption of other fish to 2 to 3 servings per week. Farmed salmon have been found to contain higher levels of polychlorinated biphenyls (PCBs) and other persistent organic pollutants than wild salmon due to contaminants in fishmeal used as feed. Microplastic contamination has been detected in over 50 commercially important fish species, though the health implications of ingesting fish that have consumed microplastics are still being studied. Small fish such as sardines, anchovies, and herring provide comparable omega-3 content to large fish with dramatically lower mercury and environmental impact. The health benefits of moderate fish consumption are considered to outweigh contaminant risks for most adults according to the majority of nutritional research.
