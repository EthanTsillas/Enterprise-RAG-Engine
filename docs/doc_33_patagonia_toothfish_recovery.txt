Document Title: Patagonian Toothfish Fishery Recovery

The Patagonian toothfish (Dissostichus eleginoides), sold commercially as Chilean sea bass, is a deep-water species found in sub-Antarctic waters around South Georgia, Heard Island, and the Kerguelen Islands. Patagonian toothfish grow slowly, live up to 50 years, and reach weights of up to 100 kilograms. IUU fishing for toothfish in the Southern Ocean peaked in the late 1990s, with illegal catches estimated to exceed legal catches by 5 to 1. CCAMLR, established in 1982 under the Antarctic Treaty System, manages toothfish fisheries through a precautionary quota system and mandatory catch documentation. The Catch Documentation Scheme for Patagonian Toothfish, introduced in 2000, requires export documentation for all toothfish trade, helping to exclude IUU-caught fish from legal markets. Legal toothfish landings in South Georgia are now MSC-certified and are considered a model for deep-sea fishery management. The current annual TAC for Patagonian toothfish around South Georgia is approximately 3,000 metric tons. Toothfish sell for up to 25 dollars per kilogram at wholesale, making each fish worth thousands of dollars. The Marine Stewardship Council certified the South Georgia Patagonian toothfish fishery in 2004, one of the first deep-sea fisheries to achieve certification. Toothfish play a significant ecological role in Southern Ocean food webs as predators of squid and fish and as prey for sperm whales and sleeper sharks.
