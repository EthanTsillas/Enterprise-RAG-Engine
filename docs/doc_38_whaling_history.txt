Document Title: Commercial Whaling History and Recovery

Commercial whaling drove most great whale species to the brink of extinction during the 19th and 20th centuries. An estimated 3 million whales were killed during the 20th century alone. Blue whales, the largest animals ever to have lived, were reduced from an estimated 250,000 to approximately 10,000 to 25,000 by intensive hunting. Antarctic blue whale populations were reduced to approximately 1% of their pre-whaling levels. The International Whaling Commission (IWC) was established in 1946 to regulate commercial whaling but struggled to impose effective limits for decades. The IWC declared a moratorium on commercial whaling in 1986, which remains in effect as of 2024. Norway and Iceland continue commercial whaling under formal objections to the moratorium. Japan withdrew from the IWC in 2018 and resumed commercial whaling in its territorial waters and EEZ in 2019. Whale watching is now a 2.1 billion dollar per year industry globally, demonstrating the economic value of living whales. Whale populations are important carbon sinks; when whales die naturally, their bodies sink and sequester carbon in the deep ocean in a process called whale fall. Large whales also enhance ocean productivity through nutrient cycling from their fecal plumes, which fertilize phytoplankton. Blue whale populations in the Southern Hemisphere showed signs of recovery in the 2020s but remain far below historical levels.
