Document Title: Fish Stock Assessment Methods and Uncertainty

Stock assessment is the process of estimating the current status and productivity of a fish population to inform catch limits. The primary methods include biomass surveys using research trawls, acoustic surveys using echo-sounders, mark-recapture programs, and analysis of commercial catch-per-unit-effort data. Virtual population analysis (VPA) reconstructs historical population size from catch records and age composition data derived from otolith (ear bone) aging. Surplus production models estimate sustainable yield based on the relationship between population size and population growth rate. Bayesian state-space models are increasingly used to incorporate uncertainty in stock assessments. A major challenge in stock assessment is distinguishing declining catch rates caused by stock depletion from declining catch rates caused by changes in fishing behavior or reporting. The collapse of the Northern cod was partly caused by systematic overestimation of stock size in assessment models that used commercial catch data contaminated by hyperaggregation, where remaining fish clustered densely and maintained high catch rates even as total biomass declined. Data-poor fisheries, which represent the majority of the world's exploited stocks, often rely on simplified methods with very high uncertainty. Climate change is increasing uncertainty in stock assessments by altering the spatial distribution and productivity of fish populations in ways that historical models did not account for. The International Council for the Exploration of the Sea provides scientific advice for northeast Atlantic fisheries based on standardized assessment methodologies reviewed by international peer review.
