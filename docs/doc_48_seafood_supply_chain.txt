Document Title: Seafood Supply Chain Transparency

The global seafood supply chain is one of the most complex and opaque of any food commodity, making traceability from catch to consumer extremely difficult. A single can of tuna may contain fish caught by multiple vessels in multiple ocean regions, processed in one country, canned in another, and sold in a third. A 2016 study published in Science found that up to 30% of seafood sold in the United States is mislabeled by species, with cheaper species substituted for more expensive ones. Red snapper is the most commonly mislabeled fish in the US, with studies finding that 20 to 38% of fish sold as red snapper is actually a different species. DNA barcoding, which identifies species from a small tissue sample, is the most reliable method for detecting seafood fraud and has been deployed by enforcement agencies and researchers globally. The European Union introduced catch certificate requirements in 2010 requiring documentation of origin for all seafood imported into the EU, reducing some IUU products from entering the market. Blockchain technology is being piloted by companies including Bumble Bee Foods, Walmart, and Thai Union to create immutable digital records linking fish from vessel to retail sale. The US Seafood Import Monitoring Program, implemented in 2018, requires import records for 13 high-risk seafood species including shrimp, tuna, and crab. Supply chain transparency is complicated by transshipment at sea, where catch from multiple vessels is combined on carrier ships with new documentation. The Seafood Business for Ocean Stewardship initiative, launched in 2019, has committed 13 of the world's largest seafood companies representing 12% of global catch to improving traceability and reducing IUU sourcing.
