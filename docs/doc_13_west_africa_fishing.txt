Document Title: West Africa Fishing Crisis and Food Security

West Africa's coastal waters are among the richest fishing grounds in the world due to Atlantic upwelling systems that bring nutrient-rich cold water to the surface. Foreign fishing fleets from China, Russia, South Korea, and the European Union have operated extensively in West African waters, often under controversial access agreements. Senegal, Ghana, Mauritania, and Guinea-Bissau are among the most affected nations. An estimated 40% of fish caught in West African waters by foreign fleets is unreported. The fishing industry employs approximately 7 million people in West Africa and provides 70% of animal protein intake for coastal populations. Artisanal fishing communities in West Africa have seen catches decline by 50 to 80% over the past three decades due to competition with industrial fleets. The European Union has signed fisheries partnership agreements with several West African nations, paying access fees in exchange for fishing rights. Critics argue these agreements undervalue resources and enable unsustainable extraction. Guinea-Bissau, one of the world's least developed countries, earns approximately 45% of its government revenue from fishing license fees. Ghana declared a one-month fishing ban in August each year beginning in 2019 to allow fish stocks to recover during peak spawning season.
