Document Title: Alternative Proteins and Plant-Based Seafood

Plant-based and cell-cultured seafood alternatives are emerging as potential tools to reduce pressure on wild fish stocks. The global alternative seafood market was valued at approximately 43 million dollars in 2022 and is projected to reach 1.3 billion dollars by 2031. Good Catch Foods, Impossible Foods, and New Wave Foods are among the companies producing plant-based seafood products using ingredients including pea protein, soy protein, and konjac. Cell-cultured fish, produced by growing fish cells in bioreactors, is being developed by companies including Wildtype (salmon) and BlueNalu (yellowtail). The Singapore Food Agency became the first regulatory body to approve the sale of cell-cultured seafood (chicken, not fish) in 2020. Seaweed cultivation is the fastest growing aquaculture sector, with global production exceeding 35 million metric tons per year, primarily in Asia. Seaweed requires no freshwater, no fertilizer, no feed, and actively removes carbon and nitrogen from the water, making it one of the most environmentally benign food sources available. Insect meal produced from black soldier fly larvae is being developed as a replacement for fishmeal in aquaculture feeds, reducing reliance on wild forage fish. Fermentation-derived proteins, including mycoprotein and microbial protein, can produce fish-like textures and nutritional profiles with a fraction of the environmental impact. Consumer acceptance of alternative seafood products is the primary barrier to adoption; taste and texture parity with conventional seafood remains a significant technical challenge.
