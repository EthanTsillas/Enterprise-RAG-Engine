Document Title: Ocean Acidification and Shellfish Fisheries

Ocean acidification refers to the ongoing decrease in ocean pH caused by absorption of CO2 from the atmosphere. Ocean pH has declined from 8.2 to 8.1 since the industrial revolution, representing a 26% increase in acidity. The ocean absorbs approximately 30% of all CO2 emitted by human activities, slowing climate change but acidifying seawater. Acidic water impairs the ability of oysters, clams, mussels, sea urchins, and corals to build and maintain calcium carbonate shells and skeletons. The oyster industry on the US Pacific Northwest coast experienced near-total larval failure in 2006 and 2008 at hatcheries when upwelling brought corrosive water onto the continental shelf. Whiskey Creek Shellfish Hatchery in Oregon invested over 400,000 dollars in water chemistry monitoring and buffering equipment to survive ocean acidification. Wild oyster populations in Puget Sound have declined by 90% over the past 40 years, with acidification being a contributing factor. Pteropods, free-swimming sea snails that are a critical food source for salmon and herring, are dissolving in the Southern Ocean as waters acidify. Under high emission scenarios, coral reefs are projected to become chemically unsuitable for coral growth by 2050 as calcium carbonate undersaturation increases. Cold water fisheries in the Bering Sea and Gulf of Alaska face particularly severe acidification due to naturally lower carbonate saturation levels in cold water. Under high emissions scenarios, the economic cost of ocean acidification to shellfish aquaculture globally is projected at 10 billion dollars per year by 2100.
