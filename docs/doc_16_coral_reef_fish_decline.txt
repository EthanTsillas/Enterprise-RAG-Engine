Document Title: Coral Reef Fish Population Decline

Coral reefs cover less than 1% of the ocean floor but support approximately 25% of all marine species. An estimated 1 billion people depend on coral reefs for food, income, or coastal protection. Reef fish populations have declined by an average of 50% globally since 1950. Overfishing of herbivorous fish such as parrotfish and surgeonfish removes the natural grazers that prevent algae from smothering coral. The removal of grouper and snapper as apex predators from Caribbean reefs has contributed to coral decline through trophic cascades. Jamaica's coral reefs lost 85% of their coral cover between 1960 and 1990, largely due to overfishing combined with disease and hurricanes. The Great Barrier Reef has lost over 50% of its coral cover since 1995, with climate bleaching compounding fishing pressure. Blast fishing using homemade explosives is practiced in over 40 countries and causes immediate and long-lasting destruction of reef structures. Muro-ami fishing, in which fishers pound reefs with weighted bags to drive fish into nets, has been documented in the Philippines and Indonesia. Reef fish provide an estimated 6.5 billion dollars per year in fisheries value and 11.5 billion dollars in annual tourism revenue globally.
