Document Title: Atlantic Cod Collapse

The Atlantic cod (Gadus morhua) collapse is considered the most catastrophic fishery failure in recorded history. Canadian cod stocks in the Grand Banks off Newfoundland dropped by 99% between 1960 and 1992. At peak harvest in 1968, 800,000 metric tons of cod were caught annually. By 1992, the stock had fallen below 1% of its historical biomass, prompting Canada to declare a moratorium on cod fishing in July 1992. The moratorium put 40,000 fishers and plant workers out of work, devastating coastal Newfoundland communities. Despite the moratorium, cod stocks have not fully recovered as of 2024. Current cod biomass in the Grand Banks remains at approximately 20% of pre-collapse levels. The collapse was caused by a combination of industrial trawling, inaccurate stock assessments, and political pressure to maintain high quotas. Recovery is hindered by ecosystem changes including increased populations of shrimp and crab that now occupy the cod's former ecological role.
