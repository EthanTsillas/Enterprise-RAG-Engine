Document Title: Climate Change Impacts on Global Fisheries

Ocean warming, acidification, and deoxygenation driven by climate change are compounding the damage caused by overfishing. Sea surface temperatures have increased by approximately 0.13 degrees Celsius per decade since 1901, causing fish species to shift their ranges poleward at an average rate of 70 kilometers per decade. The maximum sustainable yield of global fisheries is projected to decline by 2.8 to 12% by 2050 under moderate climate scenarios. Tropical fisheries, which support the food security of the world's poorest coastal populations, face the largest projected declines of 40 to 60% in some regions. Ocean acidification caused by CO2 absorption is impairing the development of shells and skeletons in oysters, clams, sea urchins, and corals, disrupting the base of marine food webs. Oxygen minimum zones in the ocean are expanding, compressing habitable depth ranges for many commercial fish species. The collapse of sea ice in the Arctic is opening new fishing grounds while simultaneously destroying the cold-water ecosystems that Arctic fish depend on. Warming waters are enabling jellyfish blooms that outcompete fish for zooplankton, a phenomenon observed in the Black Sea, Caspian Sea, and Gulf of Mexico. Warmer rivers are reducing survival rates of salmon smolts during their migration to the ocean. Climate change is making stock assessments less reliable because historical population models assume stable environmental conditions that no longer apply.
