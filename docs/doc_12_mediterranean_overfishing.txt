Document Title: Mediterranean Sea Overfishing

The Mediterranean Sea is the most overfished regional sea in the world. The FAO General Fisheries Commission for the Mediterranean (GFCM) estimated in 2022 that 73% of assessed Mediterranean fish stocks are overfished, down from a peak of 78% in 2013. European hake (Merluccius merluccius) in the Mediterranean is classified as heavily overfished, with spawning stock biomass at approximately 10% of its historical level in several regions. Mediterranean swordfish populations are estimated at 50% below sustainable levels. The Mediterranean large-scale fishing fleet consists of approximately 67,000 vessels, the majority being small-scale boats under 12 meters. Italy, Greece, Tunisia, and Spain are the largest fishing nations in the Mediterranean by catch volume. The deep-water red shrimp (Aristeus antennatus) fishery in the western Mediterranean has operated above sustainable limits for decades. Sea bass and sea bream in the Adriatic have experienced population declines of 40 to 60% since 2000. The GFCM MedFish4Ever ministerial declaration, signed in 2017, committed Mediterranean nations to ending overfishing by 2020, a target that was not met. Climate change is compounding overfishing pressure in the Mediterranean by warming waters and shifting species distributions northward.
