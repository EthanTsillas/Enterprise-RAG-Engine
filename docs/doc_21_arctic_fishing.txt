Document Title: Arctic Fishing and Ocean Governance

The Arctic Ocean is the last major ocean largely free from industrial fishing due to its extreme conditions and sea ice cover. As climate change melts Arctic sea ice, commercial fishing is becoming feasible in previously inaccessible areas. In 2018, nine nations including the United States, Russia, Canada, Norway, and China signed the Agreement to Prevent Unregulated High Seas Fisheries in the Central Arctic Ocean, creating a 16-year moratorium on commercial fishing in the central Arctic high seas. The moratorium covers approximately 2.8 million square kilometers of international waters and represents the first proactive ban on fishing in a region before it has been commercially exploited. Arctic cod (Boreogadus saida) and capelin are keystone species in the Arctic food web, serving as primary prey for beluga whales, ringed seals, and seabirds. Greenland halibut (turbot) is the primary commercial species in the eastern Arctic and is managed jointly by Canada and Greenland. Russia is the dominant fishing nation in the western Arctic, with cod and crab fisheries in the Barents Sea. Snow crab has expanded dramatically into the Barents Sea as waters warm, triggering disputes between Norway and the European Union over fishing rights. Arctic fisheries will require entirely new management frameworks as ecosystems reorganize under climate change. Indigenous Arctic communities have subsistence fishing rights that must be incorporated into any new Arctic fisheries governance framework.
