Document Title: Ocean Plastic Pollution and Fisheries Interaction

Ocean plastic pollution and fisheries interact in multiple ways, from entanglement of marine life to microplastic contamination of seafood. An estimated 8 to 12 million metric tons of plastic enter the ocean annually. The fishing industry is itself a significant source of ocean plastic, responsible for approximately 10% of ocean plastic by mass, primarily through lost fishing gear. The Great Pacific Garbage Patch, the largest accumulation of ocean plastic, is estimated at 80,000 metric tons; approximately 46% of this mass is discarded fishing nets. Microplastics, particles smaller than 5 millimeters, have been found in the digestive systems of over 220 marine species including tuna, shark, and oysters. Plastic absorbs persistent organic pollutants from seawater, concentrating them before ingestion by marine animals. Abandoned plastic ghost nets kill an estimated 100,000 marine mammals and sea turtles annually. The Ocean Cleanup project has removed over 10 million kilograms of plastic from the Great Pacific Garbage Patch and rivers using passive collection systems. Deposit return schemes and extended producer responsibility laws in Europe have reduced some categories of marine plastic litter, but fishing gear remains largely unaddressed by consumer plastic policy. Compostable and biodegradable alternatives to conventional fishing gear are under development but face challenges of durability, cost, and fishing performance comparable to conventional gear.
