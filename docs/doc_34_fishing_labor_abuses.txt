Document Title: Forced Labor and Human Rights Abuses in Fishing

The fishing industry has documented cases of forced labor, debt bondage, and human trafficking, particularly on distant-water fishing vessels. A 2015 Associated Press investigation exposed widespread forced labor on Thai fishing boats supplying major US and European supermarkets, triggering significant supply chain reforms. An estimated 128,000 workers in the Thai fishing industry have experienced labor rights violations according to the International Labour Organization. Fishing vessels at sea for months at a time can operate with minimal oversight, creating conditions where labor abuses are difficult to detect. Indonesia, Myanmar, and Cambodia are primary source countries for trafficked fishing laborers in Southeast Asia. Transshipment at sea, where fishing vessels transfer their catch to carrier vessels without returning to port, allows vessels to remain at sea indefinitely and undermines labor and fisheries inspections. The ILO Work in Fishing Convention (C188), which came into force in 2017, sets international standards for working conditions in the fishing industry, but few major fishing nations have ratified it. Human Rights Watch documented cases of Ghanaian fishers being sold into forced labor on Lake Volta in West Africa in 2015. The US Tariff Act allows Customs and Border Protection to ban imports produced with forced labor, including seafood. Several seafood companies including Thai Union have implemented third-party auditing programs in response to forced labor concerns, with mixed results.
