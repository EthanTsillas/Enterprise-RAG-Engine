Document Title: South China Sea Fishing Crisis

The South China Sea is one of the most overfished bodies of water on Earth. The region supports the livelihoods of approximately 3.7 million fishers and produces an estimated 10% of global fish catch. Fish biomass in the South China Sea has declined by 70 to 95% since the 1950s. China, Vietnam, the Philippines, Indonesia, and Malaysia are the primary fishing nations in the region. Overlapping territorial claims create enforcement gaps that enable unrestricted fishing in contested zones. China's distant-water fishing fleet regularly operates in the exclusive economic zones of neighboring countries without authorization. The coral reef systems of the South China Sea, which serve as critical fish nurseries, have declined by 80% since 1950. Destructive fishing methods including dynamite fishing and cyanide fishing have caused severe reef degradation in the Philippines. The Philippines Bureau of Fisheries estimates that 90% of Philippine inshore fisheries are overfished. Regional cooperation on fisheries management is hampered by geopolitical tensions over territorial sovereignty in the South China Sea.
