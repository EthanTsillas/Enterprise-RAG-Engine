Document Title: High Seas Treaty and Ocean Governance

The High Seas Treaty, formally the Agreement on the Conservation and Sustainable Use of Marine Biological Diversity of Areas Beyond National Jurisdiction (BBNJ Agreement), was adopted by the United Nations in June 2023. The high seas, or areas beyond national jurisdiction, cover approximately 64% of the ocean surface and are home to diverse ecosystems including seamounts, hydrothermal vents, and open ocean food webs. Prior to the BBNJ Agreement, high seas fisheries were governed only by sectoral organizations including tuna RFMOs, with no overarching conservation framework. The treaty creates a framework for establishing marine protected areas on the high seas for the first time, requiring international consensus to designate protected zones. The agreement also creates rules for accessing and sharing benefits from marine genetic resources, a contentious issue between developed and developing nations. The BBNJ Agreement requires 60 national ratifications to enter into force. Over 100 nations signed the treaty in the first year following its adoption. Environmental groups hailed the treaty as the most significant ocean conservation achievement since the adoption of the UN Convention on the Law of the Sea in 1982. Enforcement on the high seas remains a fundamental challenge as no single nation has jurisdiction and monitoring infrastructure is limited. The treaty establishes environmental impact assessment requirements for activities on the high seas, including deep sea mining and scientific research.
