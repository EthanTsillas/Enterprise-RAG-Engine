Document Title: China Distant Water Fishing Fleet

China operates the world's largest distant-water fishing fleet, estimated at between 3,000 vessels by official Chinese figures and up to 17,000 vessels by independent analyses using satellite data. China's distant-water fleet operates in every ocean, with significant presence off West Africa, South America, and in the Pacific. Chinese distant-water fishing production is officially reported at approximately 2.3 million metric tons per year, though independent estimates suggest actual catches may be 4 to 5 times higher. China provides approximately 7.3 billion dollars per year in fishing subsidies, the largest of any nation. Chinese vessels have been documented fishing illegally in the exclusive economic zones of Ecuador, Argentina, North Korea, and multiple West African nations. The Chinese fishing fleet frequently congregates in large numbers near the Galapagos Islands Marine Reserve, creating significant conservation concern for the reserve's unique ecosystem. Argentina's coast guard sank a Chinese fishing vessel in 2016 after it rammed a coast guard patrol boat while fishing illegally in Argentine waters. China's government has invested in expanding its fishing fleet capacity through low-interest loans, subsidized fuel, and payment of access fees to foreign governments. The Overseas Fisheries Foundation of the Republic of China and similar organizations provide infrastructure including fishing ports and processing facilities in developing nations to support fleet operations. China has pledged to reform its distant-water fishing industry including stricter vessel registration and penalty systems, though independent verification of implementation is limited.
