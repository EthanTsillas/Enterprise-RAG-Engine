Document Title: Seagrass and Mangrove Habitats as Fish Nurseries

Seagrass meadows and mangrove forests are critical nursery habitats for commercially important fish species. Approximately 80% of commercially harvested fish species spend part of their life cycle in mangrove or seagrass habitats. Global mangrove coverage has declined by approximately 35% since 1980, primarily due to aquaculture development, coastal development, and deforestation. Seagrass meadows have declined by 35% globally since the 1930s due to water pollution, coastal development, and ocean warming. The annual economic value of mangroves as fish nurseries is estimated at 57,000 to 90,000 dollars per hectare. Indonesia holds the largest area of mangrove forest in the world at approximately 3.36 million hectares, but has lost over 40% of its original coverage. The Sundarbans mangrove forest spanning India and Bangladesh is the world's largest mangrove ecosystem, covering approximately 10,000 square kilometers, and supports significant tiger and dolphin populations alongside fisheries. Mangrove restoration programs in the Philippines have shown that replanted areas can recover nursery function within 5 to 10 years if appropriate species are selected and community protection is maintained. Countries with intact mangrove systems have 3.5 times higher adjacent reef fish biomass than countries that have degraded their mangroves. Carbon sequestration in mangroves and seagrasses (blue carbon) is being explored as a mechanism to fund their conservation through carbon markets.
