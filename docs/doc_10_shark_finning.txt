Document Title: Shark Finning and Shark Population Decline

Shark finning is the practice of removing a shark's fins at sea and discarding the body, driven by demand for shark fin soup in East and Southeast Asia. An estimated 73 to 100 million sharks are killed annually, with finning being a primary driver of mortality. Over 30% of shark species are classified as threatened with extinction by the IUCN Red List. Oceanic shark and ray populations have declined by 71.1% since 1970 due to overfishing. The scalloped hammerhead shark population in the Atlantic has declined by 98% since 1986. The whale shark, the world's largest fish at up to 18 meters in length, is classified as Endangered on the IUCN Red List. Shark fins sell for up to 600 dollars per kilogram in Hong Kong markets, making them among the most valuable seafood products by weight. The United States banned shark finning in its waters in 2000 and banned the sale and trade of shark fins in 2023. The European Union banned shark finning in 2003 but enforcement was inconsistent until stricter regulations were adopted in 2013. Sharks play a critical ecological role as apex predators; their removal destabilizes reef ecosystems and causes trophic cascades affecting entire marine food webs.
