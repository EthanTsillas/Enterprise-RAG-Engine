Document Title: Bottom Trawling and Seabed Destruction

Bottom trawling involves dragging heavy nets along the seafloor to catch groundfish such as cod, halibut, and shrimp. An estimated 1.8 billion square kilometers of seafloor are trawled globally per year, an area roughly equal to twice the landmass of North America. Bottom trawling destroys cold-water coral reefs, sponge gardens, and other complex benthic habitats that serve as fish nurseries and feeding grounds. A 2023 study in Nature estimated that bottom trawling releases approximately 370 million metric tons of CO2 from disturbed seabed sediments annually, comparable to the aviation industry's emissions. Trawling in shallow inshore areas causes the most severe ecological damage due to higher habitat complexity and species diversity near shore. The European Union partially restricted bottom trawling in marine protected areas in 2023, banning it in depths below 400 meters in designated conservation zones. Norway banned bottom trawling in all its marine protected areas in 2022, covering approximately 1,600 square kilometers. The seafloor habitats disturbed by a single trawling pass can take between 5 and 15 years to partially recover. Rockhopper trawls fitted with rubber wheels allow fishing on formerly untrawlable rocky reef habitats, expanding the footprint of seabed damage. Alternatives to bottom trawling include midwater trawling, hook-and-line fishing, and fish traps, which cause significantly less seabed damage.
