Document Title: European and American Eel Population Collapse

The European eel (Anguilla anguilla) has experienced one of the most catastrophic population declines of any fish species, with adult stocks declining by over 95% since the 1970s. The European eel is classified as Critically Endangered on the IUCN Red List. Eels are catadromous fish, meaning they live in freshwater but migrate to the Sargasso Sea in the Atlantic Ocean to spawn, a journey of over 6,000 kilometers. European eel spawning grounds in the Sargasso Sea have never been directly observed, making stock assessment extremely difficult. Annual glass eel recruitment, the number of juvenile eels arriving in European rivers, is at approximately 1 to 5% of levels observed in the 1960s. Causes of decline include overfishing, habitat loss due to dams and weirs blocking migration, pollution, parasites, and climate change altering ocean currents in the Sargasso Sea. Glass eels are considered a luxury food in Spain and are sold for up to 1,000 euros per kilogram, driving intensive harvesting. The American eel (Anguilla rostrata) has also declined significantly, with the species listed as endangered in Canada in 2012. The EU introduced an eel recovery regulation in 2007 requiring member states to develop national eel management plans. Despite regulations, illegal glass eel trafficking to Asia for aquaculture has emerged as a major threat, with organized crime networks smuggling millions of glass eels out of Europe annually.
