Document Title: Bycatch Statistics and Impact

Bycatch refers to non-target species caught incidentally during fishing operations. Global bycatch is estimated at 38.5 million metric tons per year, representing approximately 40% of all marine catch. Shrimp trawling produces some of the highest bycatch ratios in fishing, with up to 10 kilograms of bycatch discarded for every 1 kilogram of shrimp retained. Longline fishing for tuna and swordfish kills an estimated 300,000 seabirds annually as bycatch, including albatrosses and petrels. An estimated 100 million sharks are killed annually, with a significant proportion as bycatch in tuna fisheries. Sea turtles, all seven species of which are threatened or endangered, are frequently caught as bycatch in shrimp trawls, longlines, and gillnets. The North Pacific drift gillnet fishery, before its 1993 ban, was catching 42 million non-target animals per year. Bycatch of juvenile fish prevents recruitment into adult breeding populations, accelerating stock collapse. Turtle Excluder Devices (TEDs) have reduced sea turtle bycatch by up to 97% where mandated. Acoustic pingers on gillnets reduce dolphin and porpoise bycatch by 50 to 90%.
