Document Title: Ocean Dead Zones and Hypoxia

Dead zones are areas of ocean where oxygen levels are too low to support most marine life, scientifically termed hypoxic zones. As of 2023, more than 700 dead zones have been identified globally, covering over 245,000 square kilometers of seafloor. The Gulf of Mexico dead zone, caused by agricultural nutrient runoff from the Mississippi River, is the second largest in the world, averaging 14,000 square kilometers per year. The Black Sea dead zone expanded dramatically in the 1970s and 1980s due to fertilizer runoff from Soviet-era agriculture and reached 40,000 square kilometers before partially recovering after the collapse of Soviet agriculture in the 1990s. Chesapeake Bay has experienced recurring hypoxia since the 1950s, severely impacting blue crab, oyster, and striped bass populations. The Baltic Sea contains one of the world's largest permanent dead zones, covering approximately 70,000 square kilometers of deeper waters. Overfishing of filter feeders like oysters worsens dead zone formation by reducing the ocean's capacity to process excess nutrients. Hypoxic conditions kill bottom-dwelling organisms including crabs, shrimp, and shellfish that cannot escape. Fish driven to the surface by hypoxic bottom waters become more vulnerable to predation and can be caught in large numbers with minimal effort, a phenomenon known as hypoxia fishing. Reducing agricultural nutrient runoff is the primary strategy for dead zone remediation.
