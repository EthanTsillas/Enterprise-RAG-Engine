Document Title: Deep Sea Fisheries and Vulnerable Ecosystems

Deep sea fisheries target species living below 200 meters, including orange roughy, Patagonian toothfish, grenadier, and deep-water shrimp. Orange roughy (Hoplostethus atlanticus) can live up to 150 years and does not reach sexual maturity until approximately 30 years of age, making it one of the most vulnerable fish to overfishing. Orange roughy populations in New Zealand, Australia, and Namibia collapsed within 10 to 20 years of commercial exploitation beginning in the 1970s and 1980s. Patagonian toothfish (marketed as Chilean sea bass) was heavily poached in the Southern Ocean in the 1990s and early 2000s; IUU catches reached 100,000 metric tons per year before enforcement improved. The Commission for the Conservation of Antarctic Marine Living Resources (CCAMLR) has implemented strict quota and catch documentation systems that have significantly reduced Patagonian toothfish poaching. Deep sea fish species aggregate around seamounts, underwater mountain features that concentrate currents and food. An estimated 30,000 to 100,000 seamounts exist globally, and the majority of those in international waters have limited or no protection from fishing. Bottom trawling on seamounts destroys cold-water coral ecosystems that took thousands of years to form. Deep sea fishing typically consumes 6 to 9 liters of fuel per kilogram of fish landed, making it among the most energy-intensive food production methods. Some deep sea fisheries operate at a loss without government subsidies, suggesting they are economically as well as ecologically unsustainable.
