Document Title: Sardine and Anchovy Population Collapses

Small pelagic fish including sardines and anchovies form the base of marine food webs and are critical forage fish for larger predators. The Peruvian anchoveta (Engraulis ringens) fishery is the world's largest single-species fishery by volume, producing up to 13 million metric tons per year at its peak. The anchoveta stock collapsed in the early 1970s due to a combination of overfishing and the 1972 to 1973 El Nino event, reducing catch from 13 million to 2 million metric tons in two years. California sardine populations collapsed in the 1940s and 1950s, immortalized in John Steinbeck's novel Cannery Row, after decades of intensive harvesting. The Pacific sardine stock off the US West Coast collapsed again in 2015, falling to the lowest level since the 1940s. The European sardine (Sardina pilchardus) in the Bay of Biscay declined by 89% between 1998 and 2016. Approximately 25% of global fish catch is converted to fishmeal and fish oil, primarily from small pelagic species, to feed farmed fish, pigs, and poultry. One kilogram of farmed salmon requires 3 to 5 kilograms of wild forage fish as feed. Reduction of forage fish populations causes starvation in seabirds, marine mammals, and larger fish that depend on them as prey.
