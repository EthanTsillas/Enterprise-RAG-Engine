Document Title: Future of Global Fisheries

The future of global fisheries depends on the intersection of policy reform, technological innovation, climate adaptation, and consumer behavior change. The FAO estimates that ending overfishing globally and allowing stocks to recover to maximum sustainable yield levels would increase annual catch by approximately 16.5 million metric tons and generate an additional 32 billion dollars in annual revenue. A 2021 study in Nature projected that rebuilding global fish populations through improved management could increase sustainable seafood production by 8 to 40% by 2050 compared to business-as-usual scenarios. Artificial intelligence and machine learning are increasingly applied to fisheries management, including automated species identification from underwater cameras, predictive stock modeling, and satellite-based IUU vessel detection. eDNA monitoring, which detects fish species from DNA traces left in water samples, is emerging as a cost-effective alternative to traditional trawl surveys for stock assessment. Precision fermentation and cellular aquaculture may reduce the demand for wild-caught fish as feed ingredients in aquaculture within 10 to 20 years. The 30x30 ocean protection target, if achieved, is modeled to increase sustainable fisheries yields by 20% through spillover effects from protected areas. Shifting global dietary patterns toward lower seafood consumption in wealthy nations would significantly reduce pressure on overexploited stocks. The economics of fisheries recovery are compelling; a 50-year analysis published in PNAS found that investment in fisheries management returns between 12 and 40 dollars for every dollar invested through increased sustainable yields. The greatest threat to achieving fisheries recovery is not biological, technological, or economic but political, as the short-term costs of reducing catch are borne by identifiable fishing communities while the long-term benefits are diffuse and delayed.
