Document Title: Fishing Technology and Its Role in Overfishing

Advances in fishing technology since World War II have dramatically increased the capacity of fleets to find and catch fish. Fish finders using sonar allow vessels to locate schools of fish with precision impossible for earlier generations of fishers. Satellite navigation systems allow fishing vessels to return repeatedly to exact locations where fish aggregations have been found. Factory ships can process, freeze, and store fish at sea, allowing vessels to remain at sea for months rather than days. Purse seine nets can encircle entire schools of fish up to 2 kilometers in circumference and 200 meters in depth. Modern trawl nets can be wider than a Boeing 747 wingspan and catch everything in their path. Aerial spotter planes and, more recently, drones are used to locate surface schools of tuna and other pelagic species. Fish aggregating devices (FADs) are floating objects deployed at sea that attract fish through a poorly understood behavioral response; over 100,000 FADs are deployed annually by the tuna industry. FADs dramatically increase fishing efficiency but also increase bycatch of juvenile tuna and non-target species. The technological capacity of modern fishing fleets is estimated to be 2 to 3 times greater than what would be needed to sustainably harvest global fish stocks.
